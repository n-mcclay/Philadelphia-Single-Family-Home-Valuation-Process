# What this project is
The phrase property assessment sounds like a clerical activity. Someone at the city looks at a house, decides what it's worth, writes down a number. In most American cities, including Philadelphia, that's almost the opposite of what happens.
Philadelphia's Office of Property Assessment, known as OPA, maintains a digital record of every parcel of land in the city — roughly 580,000 of them. For each parcel, OPA stores administrative information (parcel number, address, zoning category, ownership), physical characteristics drawn from prior inspections and aerial imagery (square footage, year built, building condition, number of stories), and two valuation numbers that drive tax bills: a fair market value (what OPA estimates the property would sell for) and an assessed value (what the city uses to compute the tax owed). The assessed value is supposed to equal market value, but in practice the two diverge in ways that matter.
These numbers are not produced by individual judgment. They are produced by a statistical model — a Computer-Assisted Mass Appraisal, or CAMA — that the city runs periodically. The model is fit to actual recorded sales: when a property transfers, the deed records a sale price, and the model learns to predict price as a function of property characteristics and location. Once fit, the model is applied to every parcel in the city, including the overwhelming majority that did not sell in the relevant window. So the assessed value of a house that has not changed hands in twenty years is determined by what comparable houses in the same neighborhood sold for, filtered through whatever model OPA was running when it last refreshed.
This makes the assessment system, at its core, an exercise in generalization from sales. Whatever you think about Philadelphia's tax fairness ultimately depends on whether that generalization is doing what we want it to do.
## Where this gets harder than it sounds
The structural problem with mass appraisal is that most properties never sell in any given window. Roughly 2–3% of single-family homes in Philadelphia transfer in a typical year. The other 97% have to have their values inferred. That inference depends on three things: the quality of the sale records the model learns from, the comparability of selling properties to non-selling ones, and the geographic and structural assumptions the model uses to decide what counts as comparable.
All three can go wrong, and they go wrong in different ways.
The quality of sale records is shaped by what gets into the city's transaction database in the first place. Real Estate Transfer Tax records — the source of sale price data — include every property transfer, not just arm's-length market sales. They include family transfers, sheriff's sales, partial-interest conveyances, transfers between LLCs, nominal-price recordings of $1 or $10, and administrative cleanups. A naïve analysis treats all of these as comparable signal; a careful analysis filters them, but every filter is a judgment call about what counts as "real." Those judgment calls compound. I have spent significant time in this project trying to work out a defensible set of filters, and I have changed my mind about what counts as defensible at least three times.
Comparability of selling properties to non-selling ones is shaped by which properties tend to sell. Across Philadelphia, sales are not randomly distributed. Some neighborhoods have high transaction velocity; others have very little. Some property types churn frequently; others don't. The CAMA model fits cleanly when the selling sample resembles the broader stock and fits badly when it doesn't. A neighborhood where the only houses that sell are foreclosures will produce a depressed model fit for non-foreclosure properties in that neighborhood. A neighborhood where the only houses that sell are gut-renovated will produce an inflated fit. The pattern of who sells is not orthogonal to the pattern of who pays taxes.
The geographic and structural assumptions are where the most interesting administrative choices live. OPA divides the city into Geographic Market Areas — GMAs — which are the actual units the CAMA model uses for its valuation logic. There are roughly 140 GMAs, grouped into 14 broader zones. These are not census tracts. They are not neighborhoods in any colloquial sense. They are the city's own working definition of which properties belong to the same submarket for the purpose of estimating value. Most outside researchers who have studied Philadelphia assessments have used census tracts as their geographic unit, because tracts are what's in the standard data infrastructure. The GMA boundaries are public but harder to work with, and so they get ignored. One of the things I have spent time on in this project is taking seriously the difference between using tracts (the analyst's convenient unit) and using GMAs (the assessor's actual unit). It matters more than I initially expected.
## What the inquiry is actually about
Once you understand that mass appraisal is generalization from sales, and that the generalization can fail in at least three distinct ways, the question of "are Philadelphia's assessments accurate" stops being a single question. It becomes a family of questions, each pointing at a different kind of failure and a different kind of intervention.
I have been investigating the family. The two papers in this repository are formal attempts to answer two of the questions in it. The first paper, written for an undergraduate public economics course in late 2025, asked whether the 2023 citywide reassessment changed the *information quality* of Philadelphia's valuations. The focus was not just whether the numbers improved, but whether the model's internal coherence improved in a way that survives looking at it from multiple angles. The second paper, delivered in May 2026, asks a different question: when assessments fail, where do they fail, and is the failure best explained by neighborhood poverty or by within-neighborhood heterogeneity in property values. These two papers do not produce a unified theory of Philadelphia assessment. They are two angles on the same system, and I have learned different things from each.
The notes in this repository are where I try to be honest about what I have learned. Some of them are methodological — what counts as a sale, what dispersion measure is appropriate, what geographic unit to work in. One of them, the third in the series, walks through a methodological error I introduced in the first paper and only fully understood after revisiting it months later. That note exists because the inquiry has had failures as well as findings, and an honest portfolio does not hide the failures.
## Why this matters
The technical details of mass appraisal connect, through specific mechanisms, to outcomes that people actually experience. When a low-value house gets assessed at twice its market value, the owner pays a tax bill they may not be able to afford and may not have the means to appeal. When a high-value house in a heterogeneous neighborhood gets assessed at half its market value because the model's comparison set is dominated by smaller, older nearby properties, the city loses revenue it could have used. When the geographic unit the assessor treats as a "market" cuts across the social geography residents actually experience, appeals get filed by the residents who know how the system works, and the appeals process becomes a redistribution mechanism toward people with that knowledge.
These are not abstract concerns. They show up in the data. Philadelphia's citywide coefficient of dispersion — the IAAO-standard measure of assessment uniformity — was around 45 in 2014 and is around 24 today. The improvement is real. But the citywide number obscures that seven of fourteen GMA zones still fail the IAAO uniformity standard in the most recent assessment window. The improvement has not been even, and where it has not been even matters.
The remaining notes in this repository walk through what I have done to understand the unevenness, what I have gotten right, and what I have had to revise.
