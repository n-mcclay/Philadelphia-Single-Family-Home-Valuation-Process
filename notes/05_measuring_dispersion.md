# What this note is

The project uses three different measures of dispersion at three different geographic levels. Citywide and zone-level analyses use the Coefficient of Dispersion (COD), the IAAO standard measure of assessment uniformity. GMA-level regressions use the ratio of the 90th to the 10th percentile of sale prices (p90p10), a measure of within-unit price heterogeneity. Tract-quarter panels use the interquartile range of hedonic residuals (iqr_res), a measure of unexplained pricing variation conditional on observed characteristics.

I used to think this multiplicity was an inconsistency to apologize for. I have come around to thinking it is a feature of the inquiry, not a bug, but it requires explanation. Each measure captures something different about what we mean by "uncertainty," and reading any of them as the measure produces conclusions that the data does not actually support. This note documents the three choices, what each measures, what each obscures, and why I no longer try to collapse them into a common framework.

## Three dispersion measures

The coefficient of dispersion takes the assessment ratio for each transaction, computes its absolute deviation from the median ratio across all transactions in a unit, and averages those deviations as a percentage of the median. The IAAO publishes target values for the COD: a residential single-family COD of 15 percent or below is considered acceptable for an "older heterogeneous urban jurisdiction" like Philadelphia. COD requires assessed values. It is a pure measure of assessment uniformity — how consistently the assessor is approximating market value across properties.

The p90/p10 ratio takes the 10th and 90th percentiles of sale prices within a unit and reports their ratio. A p90/p10 of 4 means properties at the upper end of the local market sell for four times what properties at the lower end sell for. It does not use assessed values at all. It is a pure measure of market heterogeneity — how varied the local housing market is in terms of what properties trade at.

The IQR of hedonic residuals fits a regression of log sale price on structural characteristics, condition codes, and time and tract fixed effects, then computes the interquartile range of the residuals within each tract-quarter cell. It is a hybrid: it uses sale prices but adjusts for observed characteristics, leaving the residual variation that the hedonic model cannot account for. A high residual IQR means the local market has substantial price variation that does not track with the variables the model includes.

The three measures are mathematically distinct. They are also conceptually distinct, and that is the more important point.

## What each measure is actually answering

The COD answers: how good is the assessor at this. If the COD is 15, the average property in this unit has an assessment ratio that deviates from the median ratio by 15 percent. The measure is silent on why the deviation exists. It could be sloppy assessment, it could be a heterogeneous market that the assessor's model fits poorly, it could be properties whose conditions have changed but whose records have not been updated. The COD treats all of these as the same thing: the gap between what the assessor produced and what the property actually traded for.

The p90/p10 ratio answers: how varied is this housing market. It says nothing about the assessor. A neighborhood with a p90/p10 of 8 has a wide range of property values within it; that range is a fact about the market, not about the institution measuring the market. The COD might be high or low in such a neighborhood, and p90/p10 alone cannot tell us which. It tells us what the assessor is up against, not how well the assessor is doing.

The residual IQR answers something more subtle: of the price variation that does not track with the variables in my hedonic model, how dispersed is the residual within a tract and quarter. This is a mix of two things. Part of it is market heterogeneity that my hedonic specification fails to capture — for instance, unobserved condition differences, recent renovations not in the OPA record, micro-neighborhood effects within a tract. Part of it is the limits of any hedonic specification: housing is complex and no set of predictors fully captures what people pay for it. A high residual IQR in a tract-quarter means either the market has dynamics my model cannot see or the model is misspecified or both.

Each measure is answering a different question and the questions are not the same question phrased differently. They are different questions about different phenomena, and the data has separate facts to say in response to each.

## Why no single measure does what the project needs

The early version of Paper 1 used the COD as if it were "the" measure of assessment uncertainty. The framing implicitly assumed that uncertainty is what the COD measures and what the COD does not measure is not uncertainty. This was too strong. The COD measures one specific kind of uncertainty — the assessor's distance from the truth as revealed by sales. There is also market uncertainty (some markets are intrinsically harder to assess because the goods are heterogeneous), there is data uncertainty (some properties have administrative records that have drifted away from physical reality), and there is model uncertainty (any hedonic specification leaves some variation unexplained). The COD bundles these together into a single number, which is convenient for IAAO benchmarks but obscures the underlying decomposition.

The Paper 2 framework, in contrast, used the p90/p10 ratio as if heterogeneity and uncertainty were synonyms. They are not. Heterogeneity is what the assessor is up against; uncertainty is what the assessor does with it. A jurisdiction can have a heterogeneous market and a well-fitting assessment model (low uncertainty in spite of high heterogeneity), or a uniform market and a badly-fitting model (high uncertainty in spite of low heterogeneity). The p90/p10 ratio is informative about the first and silent on the second.

The corrected version of Paper 1 in this repository — implemented in analysis/06 — uses the IQR of hedonic residuals as a tract-quarter measure of unexplained price variation. This is the measure best matched to the substantive question Paper 1 was trying to ask: how much variation in property prices within a small area survives our best attempt to account for it with observable characteristics. The choice is appropriate to the question. But residual IQR has its own limitations: it inherits whatever specification choices I made in the hedonic, and a different hedonic with different characteristics would produce a different residual IQR series.
Each measure is fit-to-purpose for the question being asked at the geographic level where that question lives. The COD is for citywide and zone-level analyses where the appropriate question is "is the city's assessment process meeting IAAO standards." The p90/p10 ratio is for GMA-level analyses where the appropriate question is "what is the local market the assessor is operating in." The residual IQR is for tract-quarter analyses where the appropriate question is "how much pricing variation remains after observable characteristics are accounted for."

## How geography interacts with the choice of measure

One way the multiplicity of measures shows up is that they imply different geographic units of analysis. The COD does not really need a fine geographic decomposition; it works at the citywide level and at the zone level because those are the levels at which IAAO benchmarks exist. The p90/p10 ratio needs a unit large enough to compute stable percentiles but small enough that the unit is a meaningful sub-market — the GMA is roughly that scale. The residual IQR needs a unit with enough sales per period for the IQR to be estimable — the tract-quarter, with our minimum-n filter of five sales per cell.

You can compute any of these measures at any of these geographic levels, in principle. I tried. The results are not interpretable in the way the natural-fit pairings are. A COD at the tract-quarter level becomes very noisy because the underlying ratio distribution has few observations. A p90/p10 at the citywide level loses the within-unit interpretation that gives the measure its substantive content. A residual IQR at the zone level produces a measure that depends so heavily on the hedonic specification that the substantive content washes out.
The geographies and measures are aligned for reasons that are partly about statistical power and partly about substantive meaning. The COD is a citywide-or-zone measure because that is the level at which the IAAO standard was designed to be evaluated. The p90/p10 ratio is a GMA-level measure because the GMA is the unit the assessor uses to define a market. The residual IQR is a tract-quarter measure because tract-quarter is the level at which observable characteristics and time effects can be most cleanly separated from residual variation. Each pairing reflects a substantive commitment about what the data is supposed to tell us.

## What this whole exercise was actually about

The deeper realization is that choosing a dispersion measure is not a technical decision. It is a metaphysical decision about what kind of phenomenon uncertainty is in the first place.
If uncertainty is the gap between an assessment and what a property would sell for, the COD is the right measure and the policy question is about reducing that gap — through better property data, more frequent reassessment, more accurate models. If uncertainty is heterogeneity in the underlying market that the assessor is forced to summarize, the p90/p10 ratio is the right measure and the policy question is about whether the assessor's geographic stratification adequately captures within-market variation. If uncertainty is what is left after observable characteristics are accounted for, the residual IQR is the right measure and the policy question is about whether the assessor's model includes the variables that the market actually prices.
These are different policy questions because they make different claims about where the uncertainty lives. The COD locates uncertainty in the assessment process. The p90/p10 ratio locates uncertainty in the market. The residual IQR locates uncertainty in the gap between the market's pricing and our ability to model it.

I went into this project assuming there was a "right" dispersion measure and the others were approximations to it. I came out of it thinking the three measures are each correct about different things, that none of them is fully reducible to another, and that the choice of measure is itself a substantive claim about what kind of phenomenon Philadelphia property assessment uncertainty is.

The institutional-environment frame that runs through these notes shows up here too. The COD as a benchmark is itself a piece of institutional infrastructure: it exists because the IAAO has spent decades arguing that this particular measure is what jurisdictions should be evaluated against. OPA reports CODs in its methodology documents and has them audited externally for that reason. The p90/p10 ratio is not an institutional benchmark; it is a measure that researchers compute when they want to characterize the assessor's environment rather than the assessor's output. The residual IQR is even further from institutional benchmarks; it is a research construct that the assessor would not necessarily even recognize as relevant. The three measures are differently embedded in the institutional context that produces the data. Choosing among them is choosing how close to that institutional context our analysis sits.
