# What this note is
In the fall of 2024 I wrote a paper arguing that administrative data-quality flags predict the concentration of extreme assessment ratios in Philadelphia. The paper ran a logistic regression of "is this transaction's ratio extreme" on a set of flags I had constructed from OPA records, plus log(assessed value) as a price control. Several flag coefficients came out significant. I interpreted this as evidence that data-quality issues were a meaningful channel through which assessment uncertainty propagated. The paper got a good grade.
Several months later, while building the broader project this repository documents, I went back to extend the analysis and found that one of the flag coefficients was implausibly large and statistically dominant in a way that didn't sit right. I sat with it for a few days, traced the construction of every flag, and realized the problem.
This note explains what went wrong, how I corrected it, and what the corrected method shows. It's the longest forensic walk in this project because the original error is the kind that's easy to make and hard to see.
## **The original specification**
The dependent variable in the original logit was a binary indicator: was the transaction's assessment ratio outside the IAAO-conventional band of [0.5, 2.0]. The predictors were eight binary flags I had built from OPA fields and the sale record, plus log(assessed value):
extreme ~ bad_date_flag + bad_year_built_flag + bad_sqft_flag + bad_av_flag + big_date_gap_flag + fmv_identity_flag + room_logic_flag + round_price_flag + extreme_ratio_flag + log_av
That last flag — extreme_ratio_flag — was the one I should have caught.
I had built extreme_ratio_flag to capture properties with a history of ratio volatility — specifically, properties whose assessment ratio in the previous year was outside the conventional band. The idea was to flag properties that the assessment process repeatedly mis-valued. It seemed like a reasonable diagnostic of administrative trouble: properties that were extreme last year are probably extreme this year too.
The problem is what that flag is actually measuring. extreme_ratio_flag is not a measure of administrative trouble. It is a lagged version of the dependent variable. By including it as a predictor of "is the ratio extreme today," I was regressing the outcome on its own lag and reporting the resulting coefficient as a finding about data quality. Of course it was significant. It was significant in the way that a coefficient is significant when you include a near-identical version of the dependent variable on the right-hand side of the equation.
In the original paper I had described the flag as capturing "persistent administrative difficulty in valuing the property." This framing felt analytically motivated when I wrote it. With distance, it reads as a rationalization. The flag was an outcome and I had laundered it into a predictor.
## How I caught it
The discovery happened in stages. The first stage was unease about the magnitude. In the original results, extreme_ratio_flag had a logit coefficient that translated into an odds ratio of about fourteen — comparable to the predictive content of log_av itself. No single administrative-quality flag should be that dominant, especially not one constructed from a single OPA field. Something was off.
The second stage was tracing the construction. I went back to the data-cleaning code and walked through the flag's definition line by line. The flag was constructed by computing the ratio in the previous year, checking whether that ratio fell outside [0.5, 2.0], and setting the flag accordingly. That is, the flag was derived from the same ratio I was using to define the outcome, just shifted backward one year.
The third stage was the realization that this kind of circularity is not always obvious. Lagged outcomes used as predictors are sometimes appropriate (in autoregressive models, for instance) and sometimes catastrophic (in claims about causal structure). The original paper had treated the flag as a measure of data quality. But operationally, it was a measure of the outcome's history. The "finding" was tautological: properties whose ratios were extreme last year are more likely to be extreme this year. True, but not what the paper claimed to be showing.
## **What the corrected method does**
The corrected falsification logit, implemented in analysis/03_flag_diagnostics.R, restricts the predictors to flags that are constructible without referencing the assessment ratio in any form. The taxonomy is documented in R/flag_construction.R. The surviving flags are:
bad_year_built_flag: year_built field is missing or implausible
bad_sqft_flag: total_livable_area is missing or non-positive
fmv_identity_flag: assessed value exactly equals fair market value during a tax year when the common-level ratio was not 1.0 (a true field-copy error, not the AVI-period mechanical identity)
room_logic_flag: bedroom and bathroom counts are inconsistent
round_price_flag: sale price is a multiple of $50,000 below $300k or $100,000 above
Three other flags I had originally included turned out to be dead in the working sample because the arm's-length filter already enforces the conditions they were designed to catch. They were removed. The CLR-aware redefinition of fmv_identity_flag reduced its fire rate from 25% to 0.0008% — almost all of the original "field copy errors" were the mechanical AVI-period identity, not actual data-quality concerns. With only two transactions firing the redefined flag, it doesn't have enough observations to estimate a coefficient reliably; the regression drops it.
The remaining four flags plus log_av constitute the corrected predictor set.
## What the corrected method shows
Across 174,470 transactions with complete characteristics:
bad_year_built_flag retains real predictive content. Coefficient +2.36, z = 2.32, p = 0.020. Properties with missing or implausible year_built fields are about ten times more likely to have an extreme assessment ratio, conditional on price level. This is the one administrative-quality channel that survives.
The other three flags are noise. bad_sqft_flag, room_logic_flag, and round_price_flag all show coefficients with |z| < 1.2 once price is controlled for. The original paper had reported these as meaningful; the corrected method shows they were not.
log_av dominates. Coefficient -1.49, z = -27. By an order of magnitude the strongest predictor of an extreme ratio is the property's assessed value itself. Lower-priced properties have extreme ratios at dramatically higher rates than higher-priced properties.
The substantive ordering matters. The original paper had presented data quality as a primary mechanism and price as a control. The corrected method shows that price is the primary mechanism and one narrow data-quality channel (the year-built field) contributes additional predictive content on top of it.
## The U-shape
A finding that survived the correction and gained sharper definition: the relationship between assessed value and the extreme-ratio rate is not monotonic. Across seven assessed-value bins from under $25K to over $800K, the share of transactions with extreme ratios runs 70%, 55%, 37%, 14%, 6%, 6%, and then jumps back to 22%. The pattern is U-shaped, not linear.
What's happening at the low end is mostly under-assessment: the assessed value is far below the sale price, suggesting OPA's records have not caught up with the market for these properties. What's happening at the high end is mostly over-assessment: the assessed value is far above the sale price, suggesting either the property sold under arm's-length conditions for less than its assessed worth or that high-value properties have ratios that are simply harder to model. The middle of the price distribution, where most transactions live, is where the assessment process performs best.
This U-shape is not something I had noticed in the original paper. The corrected pipeline made it visible because the analysis was no longer dominated by the spurious extreme_ratio_flag coefficient.
## What this whole exercise was actually about
The deeper realization, which I want to be honest about, is that the original mistake was not just a circularity in a regression. It was a category error about what "data quality" means in a setting like this.
I had treated data-quality flags as if they were independent measurements of an institution's record-keeping practices. This property has a missing year_built field, therefore something has gone wrong administratively. In a static, well-understood data system, that framing would be roughly correct. But Philadelphia's Office of Property Assessment is not a static data system. It is an active institution that produces, validates, reprocesses, and republishes records on an ongoing basis. The fields it populates, the validation rules it applies, the property classes it tracks — all of these have been changing across the period this project covers.
When I treat bad_year_built_flag as a measure of data quality, I am really treating it as a measure of whatever fraction of properties in OPA's records currently have a missing year_built field. That fraction is itself a function of how the institution operates. It changes when OPA contracts an external vendor for data collection (as it did before the tax year 2023 reassessment). It changes when sales validation procedures are tightened. It changes when the CAMA system is implemented and the underlying database moves to a new schema. The flag is an output of the institution as much as it is an input to my analysis.
The original extreme_ratio_flag was an extreme version of this confusion — a regressor that was structurally the outcome shifted back one year. But every flag in the original taxonomy has some version of the same issue. They are all measurements of the institutional record at the moment of measurement, not measurements of objective property facts. Treating them as if they were the latter is what made the original paper's framing feel cleaner than the data actually warrants.
The corrected method handles the circularity issue. It does not fully handle the deeper institutional issue. The right framing for the corrected falsification logit is something like: "in Philadelphia's administrative records as currently published, lower-priced properties are far more likely to have extreme assessment ratios, and one specific kind of record incompleteness — missing year-built fields — adds further predictive content." That's a defensible claim, scoped appropriately to what the data can support. It is also a more modest claim than the original paper made.
