# What this note is
Earlier in 2025 I started a second paper on the Philadelphia data. The thesis was modest in scope and ambitious in framing: that the geographic concentration of extreme assessment ratios is not primarily explained by neighborhood poverty, as is sometimes assumed, but by within-neighborhood price heterogeneity. The paper is called "Fixed Boundaries, Structural Regressivity." This note documents what the corrected analysis of that thesis shows.
The "Fixed Boundaries" in the title needs explaining first because it has been misread. It does not refer to boundaries being temporally fixed across the period of the study — they are not, and we have spent enough time in earlier notes establishing that the boundaries of the administrative geography change between reassessments. The phrase refers instead to the fact that the unit of analysis is fixed by OPA's administrative geography rather than by something theoretically motivated. Most prior work on assessment equity in Philadelphia uses census tracts, which were drawn for demographic accounting and have no particular reason to align with how the city values property. OPA uses Geographic Market Areas (GMAs), which were drawn precisely to align with how the city values property. The paper takes OPA's geography at face value and asks what the data looks like when you do.
## The substantive question
Two competing stories can explain why some Philadelphia neighborhoods have many transactions whose assessment ratios fall outside the IAAO-conventional band of [0.5, 2.0] while other neighborhoods have few.
The first story is about price level. Lower-priced neighborhoods are systematically harder to assess. Their housing stock is older, more heterogeneous in condition, more often modified outside of permit, and so on. The assessor's standard tools work worse in these neighborhoods because the inputs the tools rely on are noisier. Under this story, the share of extreme ratios in a neighborhood should track inversely with the neighborhood's median sale price. The policy implication is that improving assessment accuracy in poor neighborhoods is largely about better data collection.
The second story is about within-neighborhood heterogeneity. Some neighborhoods have housing stock that varies widely in price within a small geographic area, and others have housing stock that is relatively uniform within a small area. A mass-appraisal model that fits one set of coefficients to all properties in a sub-market will perform well in the uniform sub-markets and poorly in the heterogeneous ones, regardless of price level. Under this story, the share of extreme ratios in a neighborhood should track the dispersion of sale prices within the neighborhood, not the level. The policy implication is that improving assessment accuracy in heterogeneous neighborhoods is largely about more flexible modeling within smaller sub-markets.
These stories are not mutually exclusive but they are competing in the sense that the data can tell us which one carries more weight, and they have different policy implications.
## The setup
I scoped the analysis to the IAAO sales window for the tax year 2019 reassessment — sales from 2014 through 2017 — for two reasons. First, this is the window the City Controller's office used to assess the quality of the 2019 reassessment, which makes our results comparable to existing public-sector analyses. Second, it is the most recent window in our panel that falls entirely within a single OPA institutional regime, before the Computer-Assisted Mass Appraisal system was implemented and the sales validation unit was created. The pre-2020 OPA was a different administrative system than the post-2020 OPA in ways documented elsewhere in this repository, and scoping the analysis to one institutional regime avoids confounding the substantive question with administrative change.
The unit of analysis is the GMA. Each GMA in the 2014–2017 window with at least 20 arm's-length transactions enters the regression sample. This produced 549 GMAs covering most of the city; the GMAs that drop out are mostly small commercial sub-markets where single-family transactions are sparse. Within each GMA, I compute:
The share of arm's-length transactions with extreme ratios (pct_excl). This is the outcome.
The ratio of the 90th to the 10th percentile of sale prices (p90p10). This is the heterogeneity measure.
The log of the median sale price (log_median_sp). This is the price-level measure.
I run the regression with zone fixed effects (Philadelphia's 14 broader market regions, of which each GMA is a member) and report heteroskedasticity-robust standard errors.
## What the main regression shows
The main specification regresses pct_excl on p90p10, log_median_sp, and zone fixed effects.
The coefficient on p90p10 is 0.0056 with HC3 standard error 0.0020 (t = 2.86, p = 0.004). A one-unit increase in the 90/10 ratio is associated with about a 0.56 percentage-point increase in the share of extreme ratios within a GMA. The mean of p90p10 in our sample is about 6.9 and the standard deviation is about 4, so a one-standard-deviation move corresponds to about a 2.2 percentage-point change in pct_excl. The mean of pct_excl is about 25 percentage points, so this is a meaningful but not dominant effect.
The coefficient on log_median_sp is -0.097 with HC3 standard error 0.013 (t = -7.84, p < 0.001). Doubling the median sale price within a GMA is associated with about a 6.8 percentage-point reduction in the share of extreme ratios.
Both coefficients are statistically significant. Both are in the directions the competing stories predict. The honest read of the joint specification is not "heterogeneity, not price" but "both heterogeneity and price level matter, and neither one absorbs the other."
To see how strongly each predictor is associated bivariately with the outcome, I also computed the simple correlations. The correlation between p90p10 and pct_excl is +0.63. The correlation between log_median_sp and pct_excl is -0.68. The two predictors carry roughly equivalent bivariate predictive content for outlier concentration; what changes when both enter the joint specification is the price coefficient (softens by about 14% from -0.115 to -0.097), while the heterogeneity coefficient barely moves once you control for price.
## The falsification regression
To test whether the heterogeneity story holds up against the simplest version of the price story, I also estimated a regression with only log_median_sp and zone fixed effects (no p90p10). The coefficient on log_median_sp in this specification is -0.115. Adding p90p10 to the model softens this to -0.097 — a 14% reduction in the price-level coefficient when heterogeneity enters.
This is informative without being definitive. If the heterogeneity story were strongly true and the price story spurious, I would expect adding `p90p10` to substantially attenuate the price coefficient, perhaps cutting it in half or more. A 14% reduction is real but modest. The data are telling me that price and heterogeneity capture overlapping but distinct mechanisms.
## What the spatial extension shows
The cross-sectional regression assumes that the residuals across GMAs are independent of one another. They are not. When I extended the analysis to test for spatial autocorrelation in the regression residuals, Moran's I on the residuals from the non-spatial main regression came out at 0.323 (p < 2.2 × 10⁻¹⁶). That is substantial spatial structure — neighboring GMAs co-vary in their outlier concentration in ways that the within-GMA covariates and the zone fixed effects do not absorb.
I then re-estimated the main specification three ways:
The non-spatial OLS with HC3 standard errors (the main result reported above).
An OLS specification adding the spatial lag of pct_excl (the mean outlier rate among neighboring GMAs) as an additional regressor.
A maximum-likelihood spatial autoregressive model (SAR), which estimates a global spatial parameter ρ alongside the structural coefficients.
The SAR rho came out at 0.339 (standard error 0.037, p < 2.2 × 10⁻¹⁶). Conditional on the within-GMA covariates and the zone fixed effects, a GMA's outlier rate co-varies substantially with the outlier rates of its neighbors. The cross-sectional independence assumption was wrong.
What's interesting is how the structural coefficients shift when we account for spatial structure. The coefficient on p90p10 moves from 0.0056 (non-spatial) to 0.0047 (SAR) — a 16% reduction. The coefficient on log_median_sp moves from -0.097 to -0.066 — a 31% reduction. ## The heterogeneity coefficient is roughly stable. The price-level coefficient attenuates substantially.
The interpretive consequence is the move from "both mechanisms matter equally" to "heterogeneity is a robust local mechanism for outlier concentration; price-level effects are partly local and partly a regional smoothing artifact." Some of what the non-spatial regression attributed to "price level affects outliers" was actually "price level varies spatially and outliers vary spatially and they co-vary across neighboring GMAs." Once you account for the spatial co-movement directly, the price-level mechanism is still real but smaller, while the heterogeneity mechanism is largely unchanged.
## What survives, with appropriate humility
The original "Fixed Boundaries" thesis was that heterogeneity, not poverty, drives the geographic concentration of extreme ratios. The corrected and spatially-extended analysis tells a more careful version of that story.
Both within-GMA price heterogeneity and the GMA's overall price level are correlated with outlier concentration. Heterogeneity has a more local mechanism: even after accounting for regional spatial structure, GMAs with greater within-unit price dispersion produce more extreme ratios. Price level has a partly regional mechanism: a portion of its bivariate association with outlier concentration runs through cross-GMA spatial co-movement rather than within-GMA dynamics. The headline coefficient on p90p10 is statistically significant under every specification I tried, including the SAR. The headline coefficient on log_median_sp is also significant under every specification but attenuates by nearly a third when spatial structure is acknowledged.
There is also substantial residual spatial structure that none of the specifications absorbs. The SAR rho of 0.339 means that even after the structural covariates and the spatial-lag term, neighboring GMAs still co-vary in their outlier rates more than they would under independence. This is not a flaw in the regression. It is a substantive fact about the data: assessment-quality outcomes in Philadelphia have a regional character that does not reduce to within-neighborhood mechanisms.
## What this whole exercise was actually about
The deeper realization, which I want to be honest about for parallel reasons to those in the previous note, is that "Fixed Boundaries, Structural Regressivity" was constructed around a narrower question than it could have been. The framing of "heterogeneity, not poverty" was forced by the literature it was trying to engage — a literature that has spent considerable time arguing about whether regressive assessment is fundamentally about poverty or fundamentally about something else. Forcing that binary into a regression specification produced a horse-race result that was interpretively unstable.
The corrected analysis is less interpretively unstable and less rhetorically clean. It says: there is a structural geography of assessment-quality outcomes in Philadelphia, the geography has both local and regional dimensions, the within-neighborhood story most directly identifiable from this data is about within-unit price heterogeneity, the across-neighborhood story we can identify includes a regional smoothing component that the original specification absorbed into the price-level coefficient, and the institutional environment under which the assessor produces these outcomes has changed several times across our panel in ways that may themselves contribute to the residual spatial structure we observe.
That is a longer claim than the original paper made and a more defensible one.
## Toward the next note
A theme that recurs across the notes is that the right unit of analysis is itself contested. The corrected falsification logit in note 03 used the transaction-level OPA record. This note uses the GMA. The next note works through what we measure when we measure dispersion — coefficient of dispersion at the citywide level, p90/p10 at the GMA level, residual IQR at the tract-quarter level — and what each of those choices commits the analysis to. The choice of dispersion metric is not innocent and it interacts with the choice of geography in ways the regressions themselves do not surface.
