# What counts as a sale
The data construction step of this project takes two raw sources from Philadelphia's Office of Property Assessment file and the Real Estate Transfer Tax records. I then produce a working sample of arm's-length single-family sales. That sentence makes the process sound mechanical. It is not. Almost every word in it requires a decision, and most of the decisions are contestable.
The OPA file is a snapshot. It contains one row per parcel at a particular moment, recording the city's current understanding of that parcel's characteristics, ownership, zoning, and assessed value. The RTT file is a transaction log. It contains one row per property transfer, recording the date, document type, stated consideration, and parties involved. To get a sample of sales with which to study assessment performance, you have to join them and then decide which joined rows count as the thing you are studying.
The IAAO Standard on Ratio Studies, which is the closest thing the field has to a methodological constitution, gives some guidance on what should be filtered out. Non-arm's-length transfers, sales between related parties, government sales, sheriff's sales, sales involving partial interests, and sales with unusual financing arrangements should all be excluded from a ratio study. The standard also warns against excluding transactions based on the ratio itself — a tempting move when you see a few outliers, but one that biases the resulting analysis by construction. These are useful starting points. They are not sufficient.
The filters I ended up using are roughly the IAAO ones, plus several others that emerged from looking at what the data actually contains. The most consequential are:
*Deed type*. The RTT records every kind of document filed with the city — mortgages, satisfactions, releases, name changes, corrective deeds, in addition to actual ownership transfers. Only documents whose type contains "DEED" represent a transfer of ownership. This filter removes roughly half the raw records.
*Sheriff's sales*. These are foreclosure auctions. They are governed by a different process, often produce prices that don't reflect market valuation, and are explicitly excluded by the IAAO standard. Their document type contains the word "SHERIFF" and they are dropped.
*Sale price floor*. The RTT records every transfer, including ones with stated considerations of $1, $10, $100. These nominal-price transactions are mostly intra-family transfers, partial-interest conveyances, or administrative cleanups. They are not market transactions. I exclude transactions with a sale price below $1,000. The threshold is conservative, but it cuts the worst of the nominal-price noise without overreaching.
*Single-property transfers*. Some deeds convey multiple parcels in a single document. The stated consideration in those cases is for the whole package, not the individual parcel, and assigning it back to individual parcels produces meaningless ratios. I keep only single-property transfers, identified by a property count of one or by the absence of a multi-property indicator.
*Consolidation of same-day same-parcel transactions*. Occasionally a parcel is the subject of multiple deed filings on the same day, typically because of correcting deeds or administrative cleanup. The IAAO standard treats these as a single transaction, taking the highest stated consideration. I do the same.
These filters produce a working sample of roughly 256,000 arm's-length transactions across 2014–2025, of which about 233,000 fall in the IAAO-conventional ratio trim and 23,000 sit outside it as flagged-but-retained observations. That's the number I work with in the analysis scripts. (The "single-family" restriction is implicit rather than explicit: it emerges from the requirement that records have complete property characteristics, since OPA only populates structural fields for residential parcels. About four in five arm's-length transactions in the city are single-family by this definition.)
## The harder filters: what isn't in the IAAO standard
The IAAO filters do most of the heavy lifting, but they leave several decisions to the analyst. Three of these came up repeatedly in this project, and I changed my mind on each of them at least once.
The first is the **assessment ratio cutoff**. When you compute sale price over fair market value for each transaction, you get a distribution with a long tail. A small share of transactions show ratios above 5 or below 0.2. The temptation is to drop them. **The IAAO standard explicitly warns against this.** The reason is that the ratio is the *outcome* of the assessment process, and dropping observations based on the outcome biases your view of assessment performance — if you systematically drop the cases where the assessor got it wrong, you can't see how often the assessor gets it wrong. But you can flag these observations and look at them separately, and you can construct an *in-trim* sample on the conventional [0.5, 2.0] range for tasks that require well-behaved ratios. I do both. The flagged-out observations are not deleted; they are studied in their own right. This was not my original approach, which dropped them. I changed it after realizing that what I was calling "outliers" was actually the most important variation in the data.
The second is what to do with OPA's evolving publishing practices. The city publishes property-database snapshots through OpenDataPhilly, and how it publishes them has changed across the period this project covers. For the 2014–2019 window, multiple annual snapshots are available, so for any given sale you can pool them and use the snapshot whose characteristic record sits closest in time to the sale date. For the 2020–2025 window, a single rolling current-state file is the publicly accessible source; it contains every parcel that has had a sale since January 2020, with characteristic fields reflecting whatever the database held at download time. The two regimes have offsetting strengths. The pre-2020 pooled approach gives characteristic records that are temporally close to each sale, but the overall match rate is lower (about 58 percent of arm's-length transactions in the working sample). The post-2020 single-source approach has substantially higher match rates (about 85 percent), but the characteristic values may not reflect the property's condition on the actual date of sale for transactions that happened more recently than the database was refreshed. Neither approach is wrong; each is what it is because of how the institution chose to make its records public. I have learned to be explicit about which analyses are scoped to which window and what each scoping costs.
The third is **the treatment of administrative flags**. Every transaction has a set of indicators that can fire based on data quality concerns: was the assessed value missing or implausible, was the sale date present, was the time between assessment and sale unusually large, was the property's square footage missing, did the year built look wrong. These flags are useful as descriptions of the data's condition, but they are not all the same kind of thing. Some flags describe properties of the administrative record (date is missing). Some describe properties of the price (it was nominal). Some describe properties of the ratio (it is extreme). The last category contains a hazard I did not initially recognize. Flags that are constructed from the ratio cannot be used as predictors in analyses where the ratio is the outcome — that's a tautology, not a finding. I built such a flag in an earlier version of this work and used it in analyses that I now know were circular. The next note in this series, 03_the_circular_flag.md, walks through what I did and what I learned.
## What the filters add up to
If the question is whether Philadelphia's assessments are accurate, the answer depends on what you call a sale. A naive sample that includes nominal transfers and sheriff's sales will produce wild-looking dispersion that mostly reflects the data's heterogeneity rather than the assessor's performance. A heavily filtered sample that drops anything with an extreme ratio will produce flattering dispersion that mostly reflects the analyst's filtering choices rather than the assessor's performance. Somewhere in between is a defensible sample, and the IAAO standard gives the most rigorous available guide to where that middle ground is.
I have ended up close to the IAAO standard's recommendations, with the additional sample-period scoping noted above and the explicit choice to flag rather than drop ratio outliers. The resulting sample is not the only defensible one. Different research questions might justify different filtering choices. A study of distress sales would want the sheriff's sales included. A study of nominal-price transfers as an institutional phenomenon would want them included. The defensibility of a sample is always relative to a question; what I have built is appropriate for studying the performance of the mass appraisal model on arm's-length single-family transactions, which is the question this project is centered on.
The cleaning pipeline lives in R/data_cleaning.R and the actual construction in analysis/01_build_master_panel.R. Both are documented with comments that explain not just what each filter does but why. The processed analytical files in data/processed/ are the output of that pipeline. A reader who disagrees with one of my filtering choices can either rerun the pipeline with a different choice or work directly from the processed files and adjust at the analysis layer.
The most important thing I can say about data construction is that the choices it requires are not preliminary to the analysis. They are part of the analysis. Pretending otherwise — treating cleaning as the boring prelude to the real work — is how you end up with findings that are artifacts of your filter set. The notes that follow assume the filters described here. If you change the filters, you should expect at least some of the findings to change too.
A second thing worth saying: the choices documented above are choices about how to handle data that was itself produced by an institution that has been rebuilding its own methodology across the period this project covers. The IAAO standard tells me how to filter sales for a ratio study. It does not tell me how to handle the fact that OPA's sales validation process became more rigorous between the 2019 and 2023 reassessments, or that the publishing format of the property database changed in 2020, or that the population of properties OPA classifies as single-family residential has itself shifted as inspections and reclassifications proceed. The filters described here treat the institutional environment as something to be worked around. The next note in the series treats the institutional environment as something to be looked at directly.
